## Key Challenges

### #1 Migrant Workers Cannot Form Unions
**Severity:** Critical
**Expert Sources:** INT02, INT05 (2 experts, detailed)
**Codes:** 3.1

**Synthesis:**
Migrant workers are legally prohibited from forming their own unions—they can only join existing Thai unions, which is practically meaningless. Thailand has not ratified ILO Conventions 87 and 98 on freedom of association and collective bargaining. Government views migrants as national security threat, deliberately withholding organizing power.

**Evidence:**
> "Migrant workers can't form their own unions... that's one of the big problems... at most they could just join some other union."
> — INT05 (HR NGO)

> "Still two main core ILO conventions that Thailand is in the process of ratifying... Convention 87 and 98... under the Labor Protection law, we still have no rights for the worker to form the labor union."
> — INT02 (UNDP)

> "Thailand also sees the migrant worker as a threat to national security... that's why they never ratified the ILO convention... if they give the power, it means that the migrant worker will be empowered. So that's why this is a way to suppress this group of people."
> — INT05 (HR NGO)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-MW
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA
**Quantitative Connection:** Directly explains WJP labor rights decline despite positive public perception (Thai citizens can unionize, migrants cannot).

### #2 Undocumented Workers Have No Access to Remedies
**Severity:** Critical
**Expert Sources:** INT03, INT05 (2 experts)
**Codes:** 3.1, 4.1

**Synthesis:**
Millions of undocumented migrant workers in Thailand have virtually no access to legal remedies. Even Labor Court proceedings result in deportation. This creates a population entirely outside the protection of labor law, vulnerable to exploitation with no recourse.

**Evidence:**
> "The biggest problem now would be the undocumented migrant workers who almost have no access to anything."
> — INT05 (HR NGO)

> "If you are undocumented, how can you go to the labor court? Even it said that it's separate... they will send you back. They'll deport you."
> — INT05 (HR NGO)

> "We have a lot here... It's like million to million."
> — INT05 (HR NGO)

> "The fourth group, the one who along the border, some may have, some may come here illegally, those work in the economy or the industry that doesn't provide good standards. So this group may not get the working condition as the law provided."
> — INT03 (Employment Lawyer)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-MW
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA (border regions especially)

### #3 Fisheries: Severe Exploitation and Deaths at Sea
**Severity:** Critical
**Expert Sources:** INT08 (primary)
**Codes:** 3.1, 1.5

**Synthesis:**
The fishing industry represents the most severe labor rights abuse. Migrant workers are brought in illegally through debt bondage to brokers, work on boats for weeks with no safety protections, and die at sea with no accountability. EU has flagged Thailand repeatedly on this issue.

**Evidence:**
> "Laborers are being brought in directly from neighboring countries illegally through brokers where they pay the brokers, they take on a large debt to pay the brokers to come to basically get their job somewhere in Thailand."
> — INT08 (Commercial Lawyer)

> "You'll see all these things where you have like 50 migrant laborers, undocumented laborers on a fishing boat for two weeks at a time. Fights break out. Some of them die... you hear stories of 50 boat going out, two weeks later coming in at 30 or 40 people."
> — INT08 (Commercial Lawyer)

> "Because they're undocumented. Unfortunately, when you hear stories about just, you know, their bodies... you hear that quite often due to work conditions or fights that break out during, you know, when they're overseas in the middle of the ocean."
> — INT08 (Commercial Lawyer)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-MW
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA (coastal regions)
**Quantitative Connection:** EU flagging and international attention explain why WJP Index captures labor rights decline.

### #4 Union Organizing Triggers Employer Retaliation
**Severity:** High
**Expert Sources:** INT02, INT05 (2 experts)
**Codes:** 3.1

**Synthesis:**
When workers apply to form unions, authorities verify with employers—alerting them to organizing efforts. Employers then use tactics to eliminate organizers before union registration completes. Union leaders face "unfair dismissal" frequently.

**Evidence:**
> "When they submit an application to form the unions, the authority will check with their employer... and then the employer knows and when they know they will start to use all the tactics to stop these people from forming unions, or probably try to get rid of them basically from the companies."
> — INT05 (HR NGO)

> "Most of the case when the workers from the union start to negotiating or protest, they end up with the retaliations by the company."
> — INT02 (UNDP)

**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### #5 Subcontractors Excluded from Workplace Unions
**Severity:** High
**Expert Sources:** INT05 (primary)
**Codes:** 3.1

**Synthesis:**
Subcontracted workers cannot join unions at workplaces where they actually work—only form unions for their subcontracting company. This prevents negotiation of actual working conditions and fragments worker solidarity.

**Evidence:**
> "Subcontractors couldn't join the unions in which they work on. They could just go and set up union for their own companies, the subcontracting companies. So that means they can't talk about the work conditions, the actual work conditions that they experience."
> — INT05 (HR NGO)

**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### #6 Informal Sector Without Protections
**Severity:** High
**Expert Sources:** INT02 (primary)
**Codes:** 3.1

**Synthesis:**
Over 50% of Thai workers are in the informal sector without social protections. Platform/gig workers (delivery, ride services) have no concrete legal protections and no benefits.

**Evidence:**
> "The informal sector. The numbers of these workers in this sector now accounts for over 50% of the total workers which is like surprisingly... when this comes to the informal sector, the workers wouldn't be able to receive any social protections or benefits."
> — INT02 (UNDP)

> "These people, they call themselves as freelance workers. We still have no concrete laws to protect them from violations and they haven't received benefits."
> — INT02 (UNDP)

**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA
**Quantitative Connection:** 50%+ informal sector explains why general labor protections don't translate to majority of workforce.

### #8 Property Rights: Judgment Enforcement Failure
**Severity:** High
**Expert Sources:** INT03, INT08 (2 experts)
**Codes:** 3.2, 4.1

**Synthesis:**
Winning property/fraud cases produces only "a piece of paper." Perpetrators hide assets in family/spouse names with impunity. Banks refuse cooperation citing PDPA privacy concerns. Limited tools for asset tracing compared to regional jurisdictions like Singapore.

**Evidence:**
> "In Thailand, if the guy doesn't pay and he goes into hiding and if he's tricky, what he does is he moves all his money or his assets out of his own name, puts it into his friend's name or his family member's name. There's very little we could do about it."
> — INT08 (Commercial Lawyer)

> "We won the case. It was an obvious case of fraud. But the guy just doesn't pay out and to this day there's really nothing we could have done for the client because the guy has no assets to basically enforce and take over."
> — INT08 (Commercial Lawyer)

> "Prior to PDPA you would be able to walk in with a court judgment and say, hey, can you check what bank accounts or how much money this person has? Banks nowadays argue data privacy concerns even with a court judgment."
> — INT08 (Commercial Lawyer)

**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA
**Quantitative Connection:** Explains property rights decline in WJP data.

### #9 Employment Discrimination Against Vulnerable Groups
**Severity:** Moderate
**Expert Sources:** INT07 (primary)
**Codes:** 3.1, 1.2

**Synthesis:**
Multiple groups face employment discrimination: people with disabilities (pigeonholed into low-skill work despite capabilities), people with HIV (mandatory testing, refusal to hire), transgender individuals, and those with criminal records (especially drug offenses, creating recidivism cycle).

**Evidence:**
> "People with disabilities... they obtain being put in the work that are imposed... the company kind of like tick box OK we are hiring people with disabilities. But actually people with disabilities might be able to do other high skill work."
> — INT07 (International HR Expert)

> "People living with HIV... they are asked by the company to check HIV before entry to the companies and when the results show that they have that, they've been refused to accept to work."
> — INT07 (International HR Expert)

> "If you have on the criminal... they will not have any chance to get into work. And that makes those groups go back to the cycle."
> — INT07 (International HR Expert)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-LG (trans), people with disabilities, people with HIV
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

## Areas of Progress

### #1 Labor Court Functions Well
**Significance:** Significant
**Expert Sources:** INT03, INT05, INT08 (3 experts)
**Codes:** 4.1, 3.1

**Synthesis:**
Labor Court emerges as one of the better-functioning parts of Thai justice. It's free for workers, provides free lawyers, processes cases relatively quickly, and judges actively mediate. Awards often exceed statutory minimums considering practical factors.

**Evidence:**
> "Labour court is free... They have lawyers who stationed there to provide free service... in general labour court is OK."
> — INT05 (HR NGO)

> "For the labor court, I think that it acts really independently. No one can put pressure on the labor court. The reason is that the disputed amount in the labor court is quite low and no one gonna buy the case for the labor case."
> — INT03 (Employment Lawyer)

> "The judges really are involved throughout the process and they truly try to mediate every dispute that comes to them... the unofficial rule is that employers need to pay more, usually more than what the law will say."
> — INT08 (Commercial Lawyer)

**Quantitative Connection:** Labor Court effectiveness may explain why 87% of general public believes they can unionize/negotiate—formal mechanisms exist for Thai citizens.

### #2 Workplace Safety Improved
**Significance:** Moderate
**Expert Sources:** INT03, INT08 (2 experts)
**Codes:** 3.1

**Synthesis:**
Working conditions and safety standards have improved significantly over 10-20 years, particularly in formal sector/multinational companies. Construction site safety enforcement is now stricter.

**Evidence:**
> "The labour situation in Thailand is more developing and already some is mature if compared to 20 years ago... the working condition is up to the standard."
> — INT03 (Employment Lawyer)

> "Back in the days you'll see construction sites, guys wearing flip-flops, shirtless, with no head protection. Those are the days of the past and these are strictly enforced now."
> — INT08 (Commercial Lawyer)

### #3 Business and Human Rights NAP
**Significance:** Moderate
**Expert Sources:** INT02 (primary)
**Codes:** 0.3, 3.1

**Synthesis:**
Thailand was the first country with stand-alone National Action Plan on Business and Human Rights (2019), now moving toward mandatory human rights due diligence (HRDD).

**Evidence:**
> "Thailand has been the first country with the adoption of the stand-alone National Action Plan on Business and Human Rights since 2019."
> — INT02 (UNDP)

> "The government would like to move forward from the voluntary NAP to the mandatory registration. So now the government is in the process of drafting the mandatory human rights due diligence."
> — INT02 (UNDP)

**Significance:** Significant
**Expert Sources:** INT02 (primary)
**Codes:** 0.3, 3.1

**Synthesis:**
Cabinet decision to allow 80,000 people in long-term Myanmar border camps the right to work—potentially a model for long-standing displacement situations.

> "If they do this, they will be genuinely one of the countries in the world that actually tried to put in place a longer-term solution for long-standing migrants slash asylum persons living in camps."

## Expert Consensus

**Points of Agreement:**
- Migrant workers face severe structural barriers (3/3 experts on topic)
- Labor Court is one of the better-functioning institutions (3/3 experts)
- Fisheries represent the worst labor abuse sector (clear consensus)
- ILO Conventions 87/98 non-ratification is fundamental problem (2/2 experts)

**Points of Divergence:**
- Overall labor situation: Employer-side lawyer (INT03) sees "getting improved"; worker-side advocates see systematic exclusion
- Economic factors vs. legal framework: INT03 attributes decline to economy, others to structural barriers
