## Key Themes Across Dimensions

### #1 Conditional Freedoms: Topic-Dependent Rights
**Expert Sources:** INT01, INT02, INT04, INT05, INT06, INT07, INT08, INT09 (8/9 experts)
**Severity:** Critical

**Synthesis:**
Virtually all experts identify a two-tier system where most social and economic topics can be discussed freely, but speech about monarchy, military, or powerful interests carries severe legal risk. This creates a chilling effect that discourages broader political engagement even on topics that are technically permitted.

**Evidence:**
> "In general, when it comes to freedom of expression, it's quite all right unless and until you start speaking about quite controversial issues... like les majestic, like monarchy reforms, like the military."
> — INT06 (Thai Lawyers for Human Rights)

> "If you don't deal with some sensitive issues or deal with people with a lot of influence, it's OK... but if you talk about the sensitivity... you can have a chance to go to prison."
> — INT09 (Civic Tech Expert)

---

### #2 Perception-Reality Gap
**Expert Sources:** INT02, INT06, INT07, INT08 (4 experts)
**Severity:** High

**Synthesis:**
Multiple experts explain why general public perceptions may be more positive than expert assessments: ordinary citizens engaging in non-sensitive activities experience relative freedom, while the full picture reveals systematic constraints affecting political activists, journalists, and critics. Surface-level stability masks ongoing prosecution.

**Evidence:**
> "The human rights situation looks good, but it's a lot worse than it looks."
> — INT06 (Thai Lawyers for Human Rights)

> "Fundamental rights are there, but enforcement is usually cherry-picked."
> — INT08 (Commercial Lawyer)

---

### #3 Progress Since Military Rule (2014-2023)
**Expert Sources:** INT05, INT06, INT07, INT08, INT09 (5 experts)
**Significance:** Significant

**Synthesis:**
Compared to the immediate post-coup period (2014-2023), experts recognize meaningful improvements: more parliamentary channels, reduced use of military courts, greater protest tolerance, and landmark legislation (Marriage Equality, Anti-Torture Act). However, this progress remains conditional and potentially reversible.

**Evidence:**
> "In general if compared with right after the military coup like 10 years ago, there's a lot of development... at least there's a lot of mechanism under the parliament which we could go through."
> — INT05 (HR NGO)

> "Comparing to the past, I think 10 years or 20 years... we cannot do that. Yeah, because the government would arrest the people who going to the street and campaign."
> — INT09 (Civic Tech Expert)

---

### #4 Structural vs. Surface-Level Reform
**Expert Sources:** INT02, INT04, INT06 (3 experts)
**Severity:** High

**Synthesis:**
Several experts caution that visible improvements may mask deeper structural problems. Institutions created to protect rights may function as "smokescreen," and political control has shifted from "warfare to lawfare" - using judicial mechanisms rather than military coups to achieve political outcomes.

**Evidence:**
> "State organs that on the surface would seem like they all serve to reinforce the rule of law... But if we delve into the result of those institutional organizations, we soon realize that in fact they are there to kind of like a smokescreen."
> — INT04 (Academic)

> "It kind of shift from a warfare to a lawfare context... there may not be a reason or need for a coup because you can play out whatever needs to be played out through the court system."
> — INT02 (UNDP)

## Contextual Factors

### Political Landscape
- Return to civilian government (2023) raised expectations
- Constitutional Court actions (party dissolutions) undermine democratic mandates
- 2017 Constitution viewed as root cause of problems by majority (INT06)

### Economic Pressures
- "Sick man of Southeast Asia" - economic stagnation (INT04)
- Wages not keeping pace with inflation (INT08)
- CSO funding crisis from global cuts (INT05, INT06)

### Cultural Factors
- Relationship-based society affects institutional impartiality (INT04, INT08)
- Non-confrontational culture limits collective action (INT08)
- Social pressure creates self-censorship beyond legal risks (INT09)
