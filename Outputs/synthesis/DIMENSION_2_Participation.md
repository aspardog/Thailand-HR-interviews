## Key Challenges

### #1 Information Accessible but Unusable
**Severity:** High
**Expert Sources:** INT03, INT04, INT07, INT09 (4 experts)
**Codes:** 2.4

**Synthesis:**
Government information is increasingly available online, but practical access remains limited. Budget documents are published as scanned PDFs (30 books, 90,000 pages), EIA reports require payment, and finding specific information requires knowing where to search. The gap between formal transparency and meaningful access undermines effective citizen oversight.

**Evidence:**
> "You go to the Budget Bureau office website, you will find 30 books of scanned PDF... if you just only have one question who is responsible for that situation, you need to look into almost 90,000 pages to find about that project."
> — INT09 (Civic Tech Expert)

> "If you're talking about the information like the bidding process or how the money the government spends... it's very difficult to get."
> — INT03 (Employment Lawyer)

> "Digitalization has brought about a lot of transparency, which irritates governmental agencies to no end. Unfortunately, it doesn't bring about accountability."
> — INT04 (Academic)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-IP (especially affected communities)
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA
**Quantitative Connection:** Explains why public perceives information as more accessible while experts identify persistent barriers.

### #2 Public Consultations as Ritual
**Severity:** High
**Expert Sources:** INT07, INT09 (2 experts, detailed treatment)
**Codes:** 2.3

**Synthesis:**
Public consultations exist because constitutionally mandated, but function as procedural compliance rather than substantive engagement. Hearings occur before decisions, with no citizen involvement during deliberation or after. Feedback goes into "closed circles" with no transparency about how it influences outcomes.

**Evidence:**
> "So many times it's just a ritual... they do that because it requires... but it happens mostly before they make the decision. The friction is we don't know if our opinion or our feedback influences any of that decision because it's just a process that happened before and then we can do nothing."
> — INT09 (Civic Tech Expert)

> "They have a good intention, they have a channel, but the problem is it's not citizen-centric because they post only long information of the bill... they may ask one question, do you agree with this bill? Why and why not? And then just a big box to let you fill free text, but no guidance."
> — INT09 (Civic Tech Expert)

**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### #3 Official Information Act Controlled by Officials
**Severity:** High
**Expert Sources:** INT09 (primary)
**Codes:** 2.4

**Synthesis:**
Thailand's Official Information Act (Freedom of Information equivalent) places disclosure decisions with officials. Appeals require year-long processes through multiple agencies and courts, making it impractical for ordinary citizens to use as accountability mechanism.

**Evidence:**
> "The one who decides that the information will be disclosed is the official. So we need to pass a long process. If they say no, we cannot give you this information... sometimes we need to spend a year to kind of appeal it to several other government agencies."
> — INT09 (Civic Tech Expert)

**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

---

### #4 CSO Funding Crisis
**Severity:** High
**Expert Sources:** INT05, INT06, INT07 (3 experts)
**Codes:** 2.2

**Synthesis:**
Global funding cuts from US, EU, and UK have severely impacted Thai civil society. Organizations have shut down, staff laid off, and activists increasingly leave the country. This reduces capacity for monitoring, advocacy, and sustained engagement.

**Evidence:**
> "All civil society organizations around the world feel the impact of funding cuts from different parts of the world, from the US, from the EU, from the UK... last year, many civil society organizations shut down. Many people were laid off because of the funding cuts."
> — INT06 (Thai Lawyers for Human Rights)

> "Because of the funding and budget... many CSO cut the budget was cut now. Yeah, this is I see the trend in Thailand."
> — INT05 (HR NGO)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-HR
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### #5 Draft NGO Law Threatens Civil Society
**Severity:** High
**Expert Sources:** INT06, INT07 (2 experts)
**Codes:** 2.2

**Synthesis:**
Proposed legislation would heavily regulate associations and foundations: warrantless inspections, dissolution for minor violations (e.g., late financial reports), forced public disclosure of locations (endangering Myanmar human rights defenders), and nationality requirements. The Ministry of Interior already selectively audits politically active NGOs.

**Evidence:**
> "It would increase grounds on which foundations can be closed... it would authorize warrantless search or inspection. It would allow the authorities to shut down foundations simply for failing to submit a financial report within 30 days."
> — INT06 (Thai Lawyers for Human Rights)

> "They will choose the NGO that quite vocal on the politics or like for example on the constitutional amendment... come time to time to ask for checking, for audit... they can do under the law but they just chose to do with a certain NGO."
> — INT07 (International HR Expert)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-HR
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### #6 Complaint Mechanisms Weak in Practice
**Severity:** Moderate
**Expert Sources:** INT03, INT07, INT09 (3 experts)
**Codes:** 2.1

**Synthesis:**
Formal complaint systems exist with required timelines, but practical effectiveness remains limited. Previous constitution allowed 10,000-signature petitions to Parliament for oversight—this article was removed. Court remains the only formal avenue, requiring prohibitive time and resources.

**Evidence:**
> "We have system, we understand the problem. We create a system to tackle those problems but in practice, it's still not yet effective in my idea."
> — INT03 (Employment Lawyer)

> "In the past constitution, there's an article that if people see that any state agency doesn't work effectively... they can gather together with 10,000 signatures to propose that to the Parliament... after the amendment of this current constitution, this article disappeared."
> — INT09 (Civic Tech Expert)

**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### #7 Digital Divide Excludes Vulnerable Populations
**Severity:** Moderate
**Expert Sources:** INT07, INT09 (2 experts)
**Codes:** 2.3, 2.4

**Synthesis:**
Digital-only processes (Google Forms for public hearings, online participatory budgeting) exclude elderly, rural communities, and those with limited digital literacy. The divide is not about internet access but about skills and time to engage with complex online systems.

**Evidence:**
> "My cousin, they stay outside Bangkok and they don't have a lot of time. They don't know how to do that. They never used the Google form before, but they want to give the feedback."
> — INT09 (Civic Tech Expert)

> "We still have a kind of digital divide in our country. It doesn't mean people cannot access to Internet to the phone, but I think the literacy when they need to make a decision."
> — INT09 (Civic Tech Expert)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-IP (elderly, rural)
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA (outside Bangkok)

### #8 Indigenous/Affected Communities Excluded from Consultation
**Severity:** High
**Expert Sources:** INT05, INT07 (2 experts)
**Codes:** 2.3, 2.4

**Synthesis:**
Mega-projects in the Southern Economic Corridor proceed with inadequate consultation. Indigenous people who don't speak Thai cannot meaningfully participate in public hearings. EIA reports require payment to access. Consultations are "fake" when affected populations cannot understand proceedings.

**Evidence:**
> "Indigenous people in Thailand, especially now we have the mega project in the Southern Economic Corridor... some of them cannot understand Thai. So when it comes to the public hearing about the project, they always ignore or they have to fake like public hearing."
> — INT05 (HR NGO)

> "The EIA report... you have to pay to get it. So for example if you want to know are we going to get this project in our area? Are we going to be evicted or not? They have to go pay at the government to request."
> — INT07 (International HR Expert)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-IP
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA (Southern Economic Corridor, affected regions)

## Areas of Progress

### #1 Civic Engagement Increased Since 2021 Protests
**Significance:** Significant
**Expert Sources:** INT06, INT07, INT09 (3 experts)
**Codes:** 2.3

**Synthesis:**
The 2020-2021 pro-democracy protests awakened civic engagement. More citizens are interested in political issues, willing to monitor government, and participate in formal channels. Outside Bangkok, grassroots movements organize around land, environmental, and development concerns.

**Evidence:**
> "This is some of the result of the pro-democracy protest in 2021. So people feel engaged with political issues... before the coup and during the Junta government time, people feel less engaged."
> — INT07 (International HR Expert)

> "Outside of Bangkok now... they also have a lot of CSO working with the grassroots movement. And they are more empowered to active on their own."
> — INT07 (International HR Expert)

---

### #2 Digital ID and E-Signature Systems
**Significance:** Moderate
**Expert Sources:** INT09 (primary)
**Codes:** 2.3

**Synthesis:**
Thailand's Digital ID (Thai ID) system works effectively for multiple purposes: e-signatures for citizen-initiated bills, linking tax information, and enabling direct benefit payments without documentation. This lowers logistical barriers for civic participation.

**Evidence:**
> "We have the application that we call Thai ID... you can use your Thai ID to link your donation directly with your tax information, you don't have to make complicated documentation like before."
> — INT09 (Civic Tech Expert)

> "To submit a bill directly on the parliament website you can initiate your campaign itself and you can ask people to e-sign on that website as well. And it links with your information in the state database."
> — INT09 (Civic Tech Expert)

### #3 Constitutional Amendment Mobilization
**Significance:** Significant
**Expert Sources:** INT06 (primary)
**Codes:** 2.3

**Synthesis:**
Despite constraints, citizens remain engaged in formal channels. iLaw collected over 200,000 physical signatures in just 3 days for constitutional amendment initiative. Tens of thousands volunteer as election observers.

**Evidence:**
> "iLaw ran a campaign collecting people's signatures to submit the amendment to the parliament. And they were able to collect more than 200,000 physical signatures in just three days."
> — INT06 (Thai Lawyers for Human Rights)

> "Tens of thousands of people volunteer to be election observers to help iLaw stand at different voting stations around the country."
> — INT06 (Thai Lawyers for Human Rights)

---

### #4 Access to Routine Business Information Improved
**Significance:** Moderate
**Expert Sources:** INT03 (primary)
**Codes:** 2.4

**Synthesis:**
Basic corporate and regulatory information (land ownership, company registration) has become more accessible to support business and investment. Administrative modernization has increased transparency in routine matters.

**Evidence:**
> "For the public opinion in general... the government tried to make it easier to get. Something like if you want to know who owned the land, who owned the share in the company, the government try to make it more accessible because it will get more investor to come to the country."
> — INT03 (Employment Lawyer)

## Expert Consensus

**Points of Agreement:**
- Formal transparency has improved but practical access remains uneven (4/4 experts on topic)
- CSO funding crisis is limiting civil society capacity (3/3 experts)
- Civic engagement increased since 2020-2021 protests (3/3 experts)
- Digital divide affects participation (2/2 experts on topic)

**Points of Divergence:**
- Urban vs. rural perspective: Bangkok-based experts see more progress than those working with rural/affected communities
- Significance of digital tools: Some see transformative potential, others see limitations
