## Key Challenges

### #1 Section 112 (Les Majeste) as Absolute Constraint
**Severity:** Critical
**Expert Sources:** INT01, INT02, INT04, INT05, INT06, INT07, INT08, INT09 (8 experts)
**Codes:** 1.3

**Synthesis:**
Section 112 emerges as the single most cited constraint on civil and political rights. All 8 experts who discussed freedom of expression identified it as a "dead end" that cannot be reformed, discussed, or even proposed for amendment without severe consequences. The law's scope has expanded to cover former kings, and enforcement depends on political winds rather than consistent legal standards.

**Evidence:**
> "One of the issues that Thai people never ever been able to speak up publicly is about the issue of the monarchy... one party has been dissolved, even they gained the majority vote because they were sued at the end by the Constitutional Court about promoting the reform of Article 112."
> — INT02 (UNDP)

> "Even we have a good counterpart at the Thai Ministry of Justice, it's still something that they are not actually able to say or to work on... it will be like a dead end kind of things."
> — INT07 (International HR Expert)

> "We have people going to jail because of the issue every single month."
> — INT09 (Civic Tech Expert)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-PA, VG-YT, VG-HR
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA
**Quantitative Connection:** Explains expert-public perception gap on freedom of expression; general public not affected unless touching sensitive topics.

### #2 Scale of Political Prosecution
**Severity:** Critical
**Expert Sources:** INT06 (primary), INT02, INT04, INT05 (4 experts)
**Codes:** 1.3, 4.7

**Synthesis:**
Thai Lawyers for Human Rights provides quantified documentation: political cases rose from 30-40 per year (2014-2019) to 800 by end of 2021, with nearly 2,000 people charged since 2020 under various laws (Les Majeste, Emergency Decree, Public Assembly Act, Computer Crime Act). While new case filings have decreased under civilian government, courts are now issuing judgments on backlogged cases.

**Evidence:**
> "Between 2014 and 2019... we represented around 30 to 40 cases per year... Then came the 2020 movement... the number of political cases skyrocketed from 30 to 40 cases per year to 800 cases by the end of 2021."
> — INT06 (Thai Lawyers for Human Rights)

> "Almost 2000 people have been charged or prosecuted under various repressive laws."
> — INT06 (Thai Lawyers for Human Rights)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-PA, VG-YT
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA
**Quantitative Connection:** 98-99% indictment rate for Les Majeste cases suggests policy-driven rather than merit-based prosecution.

### #3 Computer Crime Act as Catch-All Tool
**Severity:** High
**Expert Sources:** INT02, INT05, INT06, INT08 (4 experts)
**Codes:** 1.3, 1.6

**Synthesis:**
The Computer Crime Act functions as broad umbrella legislation capturing online speech. Originally intended for cybercrime, it is selectively applied against political critics for posts, shares, and reposts. Combined with criminal defamation, it creates dual civil-criminal prosecution risk for online expression.

**Evidence:**
> "You have this Computer Crime Act which is increasingly being used and you can be held accountable for something you say, something you repost and face criminal charges and jail time without there being a very clear reason other than you've spoken up against the establishment."
> — INT02 (UNDP)

> "If you look up today's or the past few months of actions using this law, you'll see two groups of people. One would be the scammers... The other hand, to a lesser extent, unfortunately, you see some political activists that are being trialed or being jailed."
> — INT08 (Commercial Lawyer)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-PA, VG-JN, VG-HR
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### #4 SLAPP Suits Against Critics
**Severity:** High
**Expert Sources:** INT02, INT04, INT05, INT06 (4 experts)
**Codes:** 1.3, 2.2, 4.1

**Synthesis:**
Strategic Lawsuits Against Public Participation (SLAPP) are used by both businesses and government to silence critics. UNDP documented nearly 100 business-related cases over 20 years, with estimates of 1,000+ government-initiated cases. Forum shopping is allowed, enabling plaintiffs to file in distant provinces. Anti-SLAPP provisions exist in law but judges never exercise them.

**Evidence:**
> "Just only the business related SLAPP action we found that nearly 100 cases so far... if you would like to also collect the data of the SLAPP practice of the government authority, it could be up to 1000 cases."
> — INT02 (UNDP)

> "Under Thai legal regime, forum shopping is allowed. So even though the person being targeted has primary residence in Bangkok, they can file a lawsuit as far away as the northernmost province."
> — INT04 (Academic)

> "They even introduced the law that allow the so-called anti-SLAPP provisions... But the judges haven't exercised those powers at all."
> — INT05 (HR NGO)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-HR, VG-JN, VG-PA
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### #5 Deep South Under Permanent Security State
**Severity:** Critical
**Expert Sources:** INT01, INT02, INT05 (3 experts)
**Codes:** 1.4, 1.5, 4.6

**Synthesis:**
Thailand's Deep South (Pattani, Yala, Narathiwat) operates under different rules with martial law and emergency decrees creating a "military state." Ordinary legal protections are suspended; arrests and detentions occur in military facilities without standard safeguards. Despite fewer casualties, people don't feel safer due to ongoing surveillance and intimidation.

**Evidence:**
> "Security law operates 24/7 in which they can't just host the protest. Basically it's like the military state now there."
> — INT05 (HR NGO)

> "For security reasons they're not allowed to wear helmets in the southern border provinces when they're riding on the motorbikes... that little anecdote says an awful lot about it's a different context and a different sense of freedom and opportunity, particularly for Muslim communities."
> — INT02 (UNDP)

> "Despite fewer casualties compared to previous years, people don't feel safer or more free. Ongoing monitoring, intimidation, and fear of reprisals continue to limit public participation."
> — INT01 (Deep South HR Defender)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-MM
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-DS

### #6 Surveillance and Monitoring of Activists
**Severity:** High
**Expert Sources:** INT01, INT06 (2 experts)
**Codes:** 1.5, 1.6

**Synthesis:**
Human rights defenders face systematic surveillance including Pegasus spyware, home/workplace/school visits by police, and placement on military "high value target" lists. Pro-monarchy private groups systematically monitor online content and threaten legal action.

**Evidence:**
> "You may have heard about the use of Pegasus spyware a few years back... against human rights defenders in Thailand."
> — INT06 (Thai Lawyers for Human Rights)

> "A few civil society actors, academics, activists, human rights organizations including Thai Lawyers for Human Rights. It was found that our names were put on the high value target list of the military."
> — INT06 (Thai Lawyers for Human Rights)

> "Privacy and freedom of expression are limited by widespread monitoring and informal pressure. Activists and civil society members face scrutiny of their online and offline activities."
> — INT01 (Deep South HR Defender)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-HR
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

## Areas of Progress

### #1 Marriage Equality Act
**Significance:** Landmark
**Expert Sources:** INT02, INT03, INT06, INT08, INT09 (5 experts)
**Codes:** 0.3, 1.2

**Synthesis:**
Thailand became the first country in Southeast Asia (second in Asia after Nepal) to pass marriage equality legislation in January 2025. The law extends beyond marriage to inheritance rights, child custody, and healthcare benefits. This represents genuine social progress and demonstrates capacity for reform on non-politically sensitive issues.

**Evidence:**
> "On 23rd January Twenty-five, Thailand passed the Equal Marriage Law... making Thailand the first country in Southeast Asia, I think the second in Asia after Nepal."
> — INT02 (UNDP)

> "Thailand is one of the few countries in the region where we're very progressive in that sense. But politically, kind of opposite ends of the spectrum."
> — INT08 (Commercial Lawyer)

**Vulnerable Groups Affected:** VG-LG (positive)
**Quantitative Connection:** Explains positive public perception on equality issues.

### #2 Freedom of Assembly Expanded
**Significance:** Significant
**Expert Sources:** INT05, INT06, INT07, INT08, INT09 (5 experts)
**Codes:** 1.4

**Synthesis:**
Compared to military rule, freedom of assembly has meaningfully expanded. Protests occur regularly outside government buildings, competing referendum campaigns proceed openly, and police increasingly facilitate rather than suppress gatherings. However, this remains permission-based rather than fully protected.

**Evidence:**
> "If you go to any government agencies, you can see a gathering of people in front of the ministry to protest for some issue... they even provide the police to help ensure the security during the protest."
> — INT09 (Civic Tech Expert)

> "With the assembly I will say the police who is the crowd control police with facilitating assembly, they get a bit better in the sense of facilitating the protest compared to things that happened in 2021."
> — INT07 (International HR Expert)

**Quantitative Connection:** Aligns with WJP data showing slight improvement in freedom of assembly.

### #3 Data Protection (PDPA)
**Significance:** Moderate
**Expert Sources:** INT08 (primary)
**Codes:** 1.6

**Synthesis:**
Thailand enacted the Personal Data Protection Act, essentially translating EU's GDPR word-for-word. Data leaks are taken seriously and major operators have invested in compliance. However, privacy protections can also obstruct legitimate enforcement (banks refusing to cooperate with court judgments).

**Evidence:**
> "We literally took European's GDPR and translated it like word for word... we actually see that there are cases being filed with the Commission in charge, the regulators, we see movements by state agencies towards respecting and fully enforcing these data privacy concerns."
> — INT08 (Commercial Lawyer)

## Expert Consensus

**Points of Agreement (8+ experts):**
- Section 112 is the critical constraint on freedom of expression
- Topic determines whether expression is free or risky
- Progress since military rule, but conditional and potentially reversible

**Points of Agreement (5+ experts):**
- SLAPP suits increasingly used against critics
- Deep South operates under different rules
- Marriage equality represents genuine progress

**Points of Divergence:**
- Overall trajectory: Some see improvement (INT03, INT09), others see decline (INT04, INT06)
- Severity: Employer-side lawyer (INT03) sees system "getting improved"; human rights lawyers see systematic constraints
