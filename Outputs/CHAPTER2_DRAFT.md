# Chapter 2: Qualitative Analysis - Expert Perspectives on Human Rights in Thailand

## Introduction

This chapter presents findings from nine semi-structured interviews with thirteen experts conducted between late 2024 and early 2025. Interviewees included representatives from United Nations agencies, international and domestic human rights organizations, legal practitioners (both employment and commercial law), academic researchers, and civil society technologists. The analysis follows the World Justice Project Rule of Law Index framework, organized across four dimensions: civil and political rights, participation and access to information, labor and property rights, and fair and impartial justice. Together, these expert perspectives provide contextual depth to the quantitative findings presented elsewhere in this report, illuminating the mechanisms behind observed trends and the lived experiences of rights protection in contemporary Thailand.

## Section 2.0: Overall Assessment

### A Story of Two Halves

The expert interviews reveal a striking consensus: Thailand's human rights situation defies simple characterization. The country presents a complex picture where genuine progress in certain domains coexists with systematic constraints in others, creating what multiple experts described as a bifurcated reality that depends heavily on who you are, where you live, and what issues you engage with.

> "It's a story of two halves... simultaneous progress and regression. A lot improving on paper on one hand and a conflicting reality on the other."
> — UNDP Representatives (INT02)

This assessment captures the central paradox that emerged across all interviews. Formal legal frameworks have strengthened considerably over the past decade: constitutional provisions enumerate rights, international treaties have been ratified, and new legislation addresses previously unregulated domains. Parliamentary channels have reopened after years of military rule, and landmark legislation such as the Marriage Equality Act marks genuine social progress that places Thailand ahead of its regional peers. Yet these advances coexist with persistent structural constraints that limit their practical reach for significant portions of the population.

> "Right now I think is fundamentally OK, but there's a lot of things we should improve."
> — Civic Tech Expert (INT09)

The gap between formal rights and lived reality helps explain why quantitative surveys may capture more positive assessments than qualitative expert analysis reveals. For the majority of Thai citizens engaging in everyday activities—work, commerce, social life—the system functions adequately. The constraints become visible only when individuals engage with politically sensitive topics or belong to marginalized groups who encounter the system's limits directly.

### The Selective Enforcement Paradox

Perhaps the most consistent observation across interviews was the phenomenon of selective enforcement. Thailand has an extensive statutory framework protecting fundamental rights, yet the application of these protections varies dramatically based on political context, social status, and the sensitivity of the issues involved. This creates a system where the letter of the law may promise universal protection, but the practice of enforcement delivers highly differentiated outcomes.

> "Fundamental rights are there, but enforcement is usually cherry-picked. We have all that good stuff, all the human rights, all the protection. It's just we don't enforce it. Unless, you know, we choose to."
> — Commercial Lawyer (INT08)

This selective application manifests across multiple domains in ways that systematically disadvantage critics of powerful institutions while protecting those aligned with establishment interests. The Computer Crime Act, for instance, is applied against activists and critics but rarely against pro-establishment speakers making similar types of statements about opposing figures. Political cases show markedly different evidentiary standards than ordinary criminal matters. In commercial disputes, processes that should take sixty days can be completed in twenty-four to forty-eight hours "if you know someone." This relationship-based system fundamentally undermines the rule of law by making legal outcomes unpredictable based on conduct and dependent instead on political alignment and social connections.

> "The legal framework for human rights exists... but selective enforcement undermines it completely. In Section 112 cases, they go beyond the burden of proof, they don't apply presumption of innocence."
> — Thai Lawyers for Human Rights (INT06)

This selective enforcement creates a two-tier reality that explains the divergence between general public perceptions and expert assessments. The majority experiences Thailand as a functioning democracy with improving institutions because they do not encounter the system's harder edges. Those who do encounter them—political activists, human rights defenders, journalists covering sensitive topics, ethnic minorities in conflict regions—face a fundamentally different environment characterized by surveillance, prosecution, prolonged legal proceedings, and the constant uncertainty of knowing that legal protections may not apply to them.

### Geographic Variation: The Deep South

Experts consistently identified Thailand's Deep South (Pattani, Yala, and Narathiwat provinces) as operating under a distinct legal and political regime that represents an extreme version of the selective enforcement pattern observed nationally. Three overlapping security laws—the Martial Law Act (in effect continuously for over two decades), the Emergency Decree, and the Internal Security Act—create conditions where constitutional protections are effectively suspended and military authority supersedes civilian governance.

> "Security law operates 24/7 in which they can't just host the protest. Basically it's like the military state now there."
> — HR NGO Representative (INT05)

The practical implications of this security framework are severe for the region's predominantly Malay Muslim population. Under the Emergency Decree, individuals can be detained for thirty days without warrant and without access to lawyers or family contact during the initial period. Military courts exercise jurisdiction over civilians in security-related cases, and evidence collection occurs through interrogation in military facilities rather than through standard civilian investigative procedures. This creates a parallel justice system operating outside the constitutional framework that governs the rest of the country.

> "Despite fewer casualties compared to previous years, people don't feel safer or more free. Ongoing monitoring, intimidation, and fear of reprisals continue to limit public participation."
> — Deep South HR Defender (INT01)

The persistence of these conditions despite reduced violence levels suggests that security frameworks, once established, become institutionally entrenched regardless of underlying threat assessments. For the Malay Muslim communities living under these conditions, the gap between rights on paper and rights in practice is not merely one of selective enforcement—it is a fundamentally different legal regime operating under permanent exception.

### Progress Amid Constraints

Despite the structural challenges documented above, several experts noted meaningful improvements compared to the immediate post-coup period (2014-2023). Parliamentary mechanisms have been restored, protests occur more regularly across the country, and civilians face fewer immediate restrictions than under direct military rule. The political space has genuinely expanded, even if that expansion remains bounded by the constraints discussed throughout this chapter.

> "In general if compared with right after the military coup like 10 years ago, there's a lot of development."
> — HR NGO Representative (INT05)

The most significant legislative achievement during this period was the passage of Marriage Equality in January 2025, making Thailand the first country in Southeast Asia and only the second in Asia (after Taiwan) to legally recognize same-sex marriage. The legislation extends beyond marriage ceremonies to encompass inheritance rights, child custody, medical decision-making authority, and access to partner benefits. This achievement demonstrates genuine capacity for progressive reform when political will exists and the issue does not directly threaten powerful institutional interests.

> "Comparing to the past, I think 10 years or 20 years... we cannot do that. Yeah, because the government would arrest the people who going to the street and campaign."
> — Civic Tech Expert (INT09)

However, experts cautioned that this progress remains conditional and potentially reversible. Visible improvements in some domains may mask deeper structural problems in others. As one academic observed, institutions ostensibly designed to protect rights may function instead as a "smokescreen" that creates the appearance of accountability without its substance. Political control has shifted "from a warfare to a lawfare context"—using judicial mechanisms rather than military coups to achieve political outcomes. The dissolution of the Move Forward Party in 2024, following its electoral victory, exemplifies this dynamic: the Constitutional Court achieved through legal ruling what previous generations of elites achieved through tanks.

## Section 2.1: Civil and Political Rights

### Freedom of Expression: Section 112 as Absolute Constraint

Section 112 of the Criminal Code—the lese-majeste provision—emerged as the single most cited constraint on civil and political rights, identified by eight of nine experts as a central obstacle to democratic development. The law criminalizes defamation, insult, or threat to the monarchy with sentences of three to fifteen years per offense. What distinguishes Section 112 from other speech restrictions is not merely its severity but its scope and the impossibility of reform.

> "One of the issues that Thai people never ever been able to speak up publicly is about the issue of the monarchy."
> — UNDP Representatives (INT02)

The law's reach has expanded considerably through judicial interpretation over the past decade. Prosecutions now extend beyond direct criticism to encompass statements about deceased kings, symbolic gestures, social media shares and reposts, and even academic discussions of the institution's role in Thai politics. The Constitutional Court has ruled that the monarchy is a fundamental element of the Thai state that cannot be subject to democratic deliberation—meaning that even proposing reforms through parliamentary channels can itself constitute an offense.

> "Even we have a good counterpart at the Thai Ministry of Justice, it's still something that they are not actually able to say or to work on... it will be like a dead end kind of things."
> — International HR Expert (INT07)

The scale of prosecution has expanded dramatically in recent years, transforming what was once a rarely enforced provision into a tool of systematic political suppression. Thai Lawyers for Human Rights documented that political cases increased from approximately thirty to forty per year between 2014-2019 to approximately eight hundred by the end of 2021. Nearly two thousand individuals have faced charges since 2020 under various laws including lese-majeste, emergency regulations, the Public Assembly Act, and the Computer Crime Act. The indictment rate in lese-majeste cases approaches ninety-eight to ninety-nine percent, reflecting both prosecutorial selection and judicial deference.

> "We have people going to jail because of the issue every single month."
> — Civic Tech Expert (INT09)

The impossibility of reform distinguishes Section 112 from other rights constraints. The Constitutional Court's ruling that advocacy for amendment constitutes an attack on the constitutional order has closed the last democratic avenue for legal change. Members of Parliament who supported modest reform proposals face potential prosecution. An entire political party—Move Forward, which won the most seats in the 2023 election—was dissolved for including Section 112 reform in its campaign platform, with its leaders banned from politics for ten years. This creates a self-reinforcing system where the law's protections extend to prohibiting discussion of whether those protections should exist.

### Topic-Dependent Rights

Beyond Section 112 specifically, freedom of expression in Thailand operates along a spectrum defined by topic sensitivity. Discussion of social, economic, and everyday matters generally proceeds without interference—Thailand has a vibrant public sphere for issues that do not touch on politically sensitive institutions. Critical speech about the monarchy, military, or powerful economic interests carries escalating legal risk that produces widespread self-censorship extending beyond the directly prohibited topics.

> "When it comes to political topics, this is when you see people start to either just walk away completely because they see no point in the risk of sharing certain political opinions."
> — Commercial Lawyer (INT08)

This topic-dependent framework means that the same speaker making the same type of statement faces radically different legal exposure depending on the subject. A citizen can criticize local government corruption with relative impunity but faces potential imprisonment for similar criticism directed at royally-connected enterprises. The uncertainty about where the line falls—given the breadth of judicial interpretation—produces a chilling effect that extends well beyond the technically prohibited speech.

> "The current picture is great but from what I see is like the downhill of the trend."
> — International HR Expert (INT07)

The Computer Crime Act functions as a catch-all provision for online expression, allowing prosecution for posts, shares, and even likes. Courts do not require digital forensics to verify authorship—simply confirming that an account belongs to a defendant is sufficient for prosecution. Combined with criminal defamation laws (where truth is not a defense) and Strategic Lawsuits Against Public Participation (SLAPP), critics face multi-layered legal exposure that can generate dozens of pending cases for a single individual. The cumulative burden of defending against multiple prosecutions functions as a form of punishment regardless of eventual outcomes, consuming resources and attention that would otherwise support continued activism.

### Freedom of Assembly: Expanded but Permission-Based

Compared to the military period, freedom of assembly has meaningfully expanded. Protests occur regularly outside government buildings, competing referendum campaigns proceed openly, and police have generally shifted toward facilitating rather than suppressing gatherings. The street presence of demonstrators that would have been impossible a decade ago is now a routine feature of Thai political life, representing genuine progress in civic space.

> "If you go to any government agencies, you can see a gathering of people in front of the ministry to protest for some issue... they even provide the police to help ensure the security during the protest."
> — Civic Tech Expert (INT09)

However, this expansion remains permission-based rather than fully protected as a matter of right. The Public Assembly Act requires advance notification to police, and while notifications are generally approved, the requirement itself positions assembly as a conditional privilege subject to official discretion rather than an inherent right. More significantly, participants may receive summonses or criminal charges weeks or months after attending peaceful assemblies, creating ongoing legal and financial burdens that accumulate over time.

> "When you've been charged with variations of public assembly act it will be difficult for them... for the next time to come to be the organizer and notified to the police."
> — International HR Expert (INT07)

This delayed enforcement pattern represents a strategic adaptation: rather than suppressing protests in the moment and generating images of state violence, authorities allow demonstrations to proceed and pursue prosecution afterward. The strategy of attrition works by imposing mounting costs on repeat participants. Organizers face ongoing court appearances, legal fees, and the constant possibility of conviction. The right to assemble exists in practice, but its exercise carries deferred consequences that discourage sustained mobilization and transfer resources from organizing to legal defense.

### Progress: Marriage Equality and Privacy Protection

Not all developments in civil and political rights involve constraint. Thailand's passage of Marriage Equality in January 2025 represents landmark progress, making the country the first in Southeast Asia and only the second in Asia to recognize same-sex marriage. The legislation extends beyond ceremonial recognition to encompass inheritance rights, child custody and adoption, medical decision-making authority for partners, and access to spousal benefits across public and private sectors.

> "Thailand is one of the few countries in the region where we're very progressive in that sense. But politically, kind of opposite ends of the spectrum."
> — Commercial Lawyer (INT08)

This achievement demonstrates that meaningful reform remains possible on issues without direct political sensitivity. LGBTQ+ advocacy operated within the bounds of permissible discourse—the monarchy and military do not perceive marriage equality as threatening their institutional interests—and civil society could mobilize openly for legislative change. The contrast between this success and the impossibility of Section 112 reform illustrates the topic-dependent nature of Thai democracy.

> "On 23rd January 2025, Thailand passed the Equal Marriage Law... making Thailand the first country in Southeast Asia."
> — UNDP Representatives (INT02)

Similarly, implementation of the Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA)—essentially a direct translation of the European GDPR—has strengthened privacy protections for personal data. The regulator has demonstrated willingness to file cases against violators, and the private sector has invested substantially in compliance. These achievements in non-politically sensitive domains demonstrate institutional capacity for progressive change that remains latent where political sensitivities intervene.

## Section 2.2: Participation and Access to Information

### Information Accessible but Unusable

Thailand has made significant strides in digitizing government information. Budget documents, regulatory filings, administrative records, and policy materials are increasingly available online. The formal infrastructure for transparency has expanded considerably, representing genuine institutional modernization. However, formal accessibility does not translate into practical usability for the citizens and civil society organizations who might use this information to hold government accountable.

> "You go to the Budget Bureau office website, you will find 30 books of scanned PDF... you need to look into almost 90,000 pages to find about that project."
> — Civic Tech Expert (INT09)

The gap between technical availability and meaningful access represents a central barrier to citizen oversight. Budget information exists online, but as massive volumes of scanned PDFs that cannot be searched, analyzed, or cross-referenced without substantial technical resources. Finding information about a specific project requires navigating tens of thousands of pages of documents formatted for bureaucratic storage rather than public scrutiny. The result is that information is technically public but practically inaccessible to anyone without specialized skills and significant time to invest.

> "Digitalization has brought about a lot of transparency... Unfortunately, it doesn't bring about accountability."
> — Academic (INT04)

Environmental Impact Assessment reports—critical documents for communities affected by development projects—require payment to access. The Official Information Act has existed for over two decades, but the process of requesting documents can take more than a year, with appeals through multiple agencies and no guarantee of success. Crucially, the official who holds the information decides whether to disclose it, creating a system where transparency depends on bureaucratic willingness rather than citizen entitlement. This pattern—formal mechanisms that satisfy procedural requirements without delivering substantive accountability—recurs throughout Thailand's participation infrastructure.

### Public Consultations as Ritual

Constitutional requirements mandate public consultation on new legislation and significant government decisions. These consultations satisfy formal requirements for citizen input and create documentation of participatory processes. In practice, however, they function as procedural compliance rather than substantive engagement that influences outcomes.

> "So many times it's just a ritual... we don't know if our opinion or our feedback influences any of that decision because it's just a process that happened before and then we can do nothing."
> — Civic Tech Expert (INT09)

Consultations typically occur only before decisions are made, with no mechanism for citizen involvement during deliberation or monitoring after implementation. Feedback enters what one expert called "closed circles"—it is collected, documented, and filed, but there is no transparency about how or whether it influences the decision-making process. Citizens provide input into a system that provides no feedback about what happened to that input, creating participation without influence.

> "The EIA report... you have to pay to get it. So for example if you want to know are we going to get this project in our area? Are we going to be evicted or not? They have to go pay at the government to request."
> — International HR Expert (INT07)

For communities directly affected by development projects, this creates particular frustration. Indigenous populations may be unable to understand Thai-language proceedings or technical documents presented in consultations. Hearings may be held in locations inaccessible to affected communities. The documents themselves require expert interpretation that community members cannot access. The current constitution removed the citizen petition mechanism that previously allowed 10,000 signatures to propose matters directly to Parliament, narrowing formal avenues for citizen-initiated legislative action. The infrastructure of participation exists, but meaningful influence over outcomes remains elusive.

### Civil Society Under Pressure

The space for civil society engagement faces multiple intersecting pressures that have intensified in recent years. Global funding reductions from US, EU, and UK sources have created a financial crisis for Thai civil society, forcing organizations to reduce staff, scale back programs, or shut down entirely. This funding contraction reflects broader geopolitical shifts but has severe local consequences for organizations that depend on international support.

> "All civil society organizations around the world feel the impact of funding cuts from different parts of the world, from the US, from the EU, from the UK... last year, many civil society organizations shut down."
> — Thai Lawyers for Human Rights (INT06)

Beyond funding, regulatory pressure has intensified. Proposed legislation would expand government oversight powers over associations and foundations, including authority for warrantless inspections, dissolution for minor procedural violations, and disclosure requirements that could endanger activists working on sensitive issues. The draft NGO law has been repeatedly proposed, withdrawn, and reintroduced, creating persistent uncertainty about the legal framework for civil society operations.

> "They will choose the NGO that quite vocal on the politics or like for example on the constitutional amendment... come time to time to ask for checking for audit... they can do under the law but they just chose to do with a certain NGO."
> — International HR Expert (INT07)

The Ministry of Interior already conducts selective audits of politically vocal organizations, using existing compliance frameworks to impose administrative burdens on groups engaged in advocacy. This selective enforcement mirrors the pattern observed elsewhere: the formal requirement applies to all, but enforcement targets critics. Several experts noted that human rights defenders increasingly leave Thailand to avoid prosecution, representing a brain drain of expertise that further weakens the capacity for domestic advocacy. The cumulative effect is a civil society sector that is underfunded, overregulated, and increasingly unable to fulfill its oversight function.

### Civic Engagement Since 2021

Despite these constraints, the 2020-2021 pro-democracy protests awakened broader civic engagement that persists in altered forms. More citizens express interest in political issues than before the protest movement, and progressive parties subsequently won all parliamentary seats in Bangkok and Chiang Mai—an unprecedented electoral shift reflecting youth mobilization. Grassroots movements have emerged outside Bangkok around land, environmental, and development concerns, representing a geographic diffusion of civic activism.

> "This is some of the result of the pro-democracy protest in 2021. So people feel engaged with political issues."
> — International HR Expert (INT07)

Formal participation channels remain active and sometimes produce impressive results. One constitutional amendment initiative collected over 200,000 physical signatures in three days, demonstrating substantial public appetite for political engagement. Thailand's Digital ID system has lowered logistical barriers to civic participation, enabling electronic signatures for citizen-initiated legislation and streamlining administrative processes. The infrastructure for engagement exists and is occasionally utilized effectively.

> "It's not that they don't care. It's not that they don't want to criticize. It's that they have come to realize that there's no point, that change is impossible. So there's a grand fatigue."
> — Academic (INT04)

However, the aftermath of the 2020-2021 movement also reveals the limits of mobilization under current conditions. Many protest participants continue to face criminal charges years later, creating ongoing legal burdens that function as deterrents to future activism. The dissolution of Move Forward demonstrated that electoral success can be reversed through judicial intervention. The result for many young people is what one academic termed a "grand fatigue"—not disengagement from caring about political issues, but recognition that the current system offers limited pathways for meaningful change. The question is whether this latent civic energy can find channels for expression within existing institutional constraints.

## Section 2.3: Labor Rights and Property Rights

### A Two-Tier Labor System

Thailand's labor rights framework exhibits a fundamental division between protected and unprotected workers that creates dramatically different experiences depending on employment category and documentation status. Thai citizens working in the formal sector enjoy reasonably robust protections: union rights are recognized in law, workplace safety standards have improved significantly over the past two decades, and the Labor Court provides accessible, relatively fast, and free adjudication of disputes.

> "For the labor court, I think that it acts really independently. No one can put pressure on the labor court."
> — Employment Lawyer (INT03)

The Labor Court represents one of the better-functioning institutions in the Thai judicial system. Proceedings are free for workers, staffed lawyers provide representation without charge, and judges actively mediate settlements that often exceed statutory minimums. Cases move faster than in regular civil courts, and experts from both worker and employer perspectives described the court as genuinely independent—unlike other judicial institutions discussed in this report, political pressure does not appear to influence Labor Court outcomes.

> "Labour court is free... They have lawyers who stationed there to provide free service... in general labour court is OK."
> — HR NGO Representative (INT05)

However, these protections apply primarily to documented Thai workers in formal employment relationships. Millions of workers exist outside this protected sphere, and for them, Thailand's labor rights framework offers little meaningful protection. Migrant workers cannot form their own unions—they may only join existing Thai unions, a right that is practically meaningless given language barriers, employer intimidation, and the precarious legal status that makes asserting any rights risky. Thailand has not ratified ILO Conventions 87 and 98 on freedom of association and collective bargaining, and officials view this non-ratification as deliberate policy rather than oversight.

### Migrant Worker Vulnerability

The structural exclusion of migrant workers from labor protections reflects both economic dependence on cheap foreign labor and security concerns that frame migrants as potential threats rather than rights-holders. Thailand's economy depends heavily on workers from Myanmar, Cambodia, Laos, and other neighboring countries, but the legal framework treats their organizing capacity as a danger to be contained rather than a right to be protected.

> "Migrant workers can't form their own unions... Thailand also sees the migrant worker as a threat to national security... that's why they never ratified the ILO convention."
> — HR NGO Representative (INT05)

Undocumented workers face even more severe vulnerability. Experts estimated their numbers in the millions—workers who crossed borders informally or whose documentation has lapsed. For this population, accessing any legal remedy effectively means deportation. Even Labor Court proceedings, which are free and relatively accessible for Thai workers, result in immigration consequences for undocumented migrants who come forward. This creates a population entirely outside the protection of law and systematically vulnerable to exploitation by employers who understand that their workers cannot complain.

> "The biggest problem now would be the undocumented migrant workers who almost have no access to anything... We have a lot here... It's like million to million."
> — HR NGO Representative (INT05)

Rising xenophobic sentiment compounds these structural vulnerabilities. Experts reported vigilante groups targeting migrant communities, demanding police checks on documentation status, and creating hostile environments that further discourage migrants from asserting any rights. The Thai-Cambodian border conflict has intensified anti-migrant narratives, providing political cover for harassment. Healthcare access, while theoretically universal for Thai citizens, operates differently for migrants who may face demands for payment or differential treatment based on documentation status.

### Fisheries: The Extreme Case

The fishing industry represents the most severe documented labor rights abuse in Thailand, combining all the vulnerability factors discussed above—undocumented status, geographic isolation, debt bondage, and complete absence of oversight—to create conditions that meet international definitions of forced labor. Workers are recruited in neighboring countries through brokers who advance funds for transportation and documentation fees, creating debt obligations that bind workers before they even begin employment.

> "Laborers are being brought in directly from neighboring countries illegally through brokers where they pay the brokers, they take on a large debt to pay the brokers."
> — Commercial Lawyer (INT08)

Once at sea, workers face extended periods—weeks or months—with minimal safety protections, inadequate food and water, and no means to report abuse or seek help. Boats operate outside territorial waters where Thai labor law nominally does not apply, and the isolation from shore means that workers have no exit option short of completing their contracts. Violence and deaths occur with no investigation because the workers are undocumented and the incidents happen outside state jurisdiction.

> "You hear stories of 50 boats going out, two weeks later coming in at 30 or 40 people."
> — Commercial Lawyer (INT08)

The European Union has repeatedly flagged Thailand on fisheries practices, threatening trade consequences and creating international pressure for reform. Some improvements have occurred in documentation and monitoring systems. However, the underlying dynamics—undocumented status, broker control, geographic isolation, and the economic pressure to minimize labor costs in a competitive global market—make enforcement exceptionally difficult. Workers who might report abuse face deportation and loss of any wages owed. Employers who might be sanctioned have learned to structure operations to avoid liability.

### Property Rights: The Enforcement Problem

Property rights in Thailand face a different challenge than labor rights: the framework for protecting property is reasonably sound, but enforcement of that framework consistently fails. Multiple experts described the frustration of winning court cases—sometimes on clear-cut issues of fraud or contract violation—only to find that judgments are essentially unenforceable against defendants who understand how to work the system.

> "We won the case. It was an obvious case of fraud. But the guy just doesn't pay out and to this day there's really nothing we could have done."
> — Commercial Lawyer (INT08)

The primary evasion mechanism is asset transfer. Before or during litigation, defendants move assets into the names of family members, friends, or shell entities, making them unreachable for judgment execution. Thai law provides limited tools to trace or recover such transfers, and the burden falls on the prevailing party to locate assets rather than on the judgment debtor to disclose them. Banks, citing PDPA privacy requirements, refuse to disclose account information even in the face of court judgments.

> "In Thailand, if the guy doesn't pay and he goes into hiding and if he's tricky, what he does is he moves all his money or his assets out of his own name, puts it into his friend's name or his family member's name."
> — Commercial Lawyer (INT08)

This enforcement gap affects both commercial and individual disputes. Companies engaged in international transactions report that Thailand "really, really lacks behind" comparable jurisdictions like Singapore or Hong Kong in judgment enforcement. Procedural costs compound the problem: a ship under maritime arrest incurs daily fees of approximately $6,000 until full case resolution, creating enormous pressure to settle regardless of legal merit. The result is a civil justice system where winning a lawsuit produces "a piece of paper" whose value depends entirely on whether the defendant chooses to comply.

## Section 2.4: Fair and Impartial Justice

### Two Systems of Justice

The expert interviews reveal a bifurcated justice system where outcomes depend fundamentally on whether a case falls within ordinary or politically sensitive categories. For routine criminal and civil matters, the Thai justice system functions with reasonable independence and predictability. Procedures are established, judges apply law with general consistency, and outcomes reflect the merits of cases. Several specialized courts—particularly the Labor Court and Administrative Court—emerged from expert discussions as relatively well-functioning institutions that deliver reasonable justice within their domains.

> "For the labor court, I think that it acts really independently. No one can put pressure on the labor court."
> — Employment Lawyer (INT03)

The Administrative Court hears complaints against government agencies with average resolution times of 1.5-2 years—relatively fast by Thai standards—and experts described it as "one of the more reliable mechanisms" for challenging official decisions. Judges self-correct, with some evidence that courts expel judges caught receiving bribes. For the majority of cases that do not involve political sensitivity, the system works adequately.

> "The administrative court has emerged as one of the more reliable mechanisms here in the sense that they tend to hear every complaint and the delay for them to deliver justice isn't too long."
> — Academic (INT04)

However, politically sensitive cases—particularly those involving lese-majeste, protest activity, constitutional challenges, or human rights advocacy—operate under fundamentally different rules. The same courts that function reasonably in routine matters apply different evidentiary standards, procedural protections, and outcomes when political interests are at stake. This bifurcation explains why surveys of the general population may show moderate confidence in justice institutions while experts who handle politically sensitive cases report severe dysfunction.

### Structural Constraints on Independence

Several experts identified internal review practices as limiting judicial independence in ways that may not be visible to outside observers. A 2019 Supreme Court regulation requires judges to submit draft orders and judgments to regional Chief Justices for review before issuance. What presents formally as quality control or consistency assurance functions in practice as hierarchical oversight that allows senior judges to shape the decisions of junior colleagues.

> "2019 Supreme Court regulation that requires judges to submit drafts order or draft judgments to be reviewed by the regional Chief Justice before those judgments or orders can be issued. So again, where's the independence there?"
> — Thai Lawyers for Human Rights (INT06)

This review mechanism is supplemented by informal consultation practices during proceedings. Judges routinely take recesses—sometimes multiple times during a single hearing—to consult with court executives about decisions on bail, adjournments, evidence requests, and case management. While such consultation might be defended as coordination or guidance, it creates channels through which external preferences can shape ostensibly independent judicial decisions.

> "It's very common for judges to take a 5 minute recess, 10 minute recess, 30 minute recess to walk out of the courtroom to consult the court executives about what to do in their case."
> — Thai Lawyers for Human Rights (INT06)

These hierarchical oversight mechanisms create internal pressure, particularly in politically sensitive cases, that undermines both the appearance and substance of independent adjudication. Judges who know their decisions will be reviewed by superiors and that their careers depend on satisfactory performance evaluations have powerful incentives to anticipate what outcomes those superiors expect. The formal structure of judicial independence exists, but the practical operation of hierarchical review creates pathways for influence that do not appear in official accounts of how courts function.

### The Constitutional Court

The Constitutional Court receives particular criticism across multiple expert interviews as functioning more as a political instrument than as a guardian of constitutional rights. Rather than protecting the rights enumerated in the constitution, the Court has consistently deployed its authority to limit political competition and constrain democratic outcomes that threaten established interests.

> "The Constitutional Court, which has proved time and again that they don't really work to protect the rights enshrined in the Constitution. They're not the guardian of the Constitution. Instead, they're being used as a political tool to curb what is viewed as dissent."
> — Academic (INT04)

The Court's political function is most visible in party dissolution cases. It has dissolved multiple political parties and banned their leaders from politics for decades—including parties that won elections. The dissolution of Move Forward in 2024, following its victory in the 2023 general election, exemplified this pattern: the party was disbanded and its leaders banned for ten years not for illegal conduct but for campaigning on a policy position (Section 112 reform) that the Court ruled unconstitutional.

> "Just a small group of people can kind of demolish everything we did during that big day."
> — Civic Tech Expert (INT09)

The appointment process compounds these concerns. Constitutional Court judges require approval from the Senate, which under the 2017 constitution was appointed rather than elected. This means that a small group of unelected officials selected judges who then exercise power to dissolve elected parties and ban elected politicians. The accountability failures cascade: officials who face no electoral accountability appoint judges who face no electoral accountability to make decisions that override electoral outcomes. The result, as one expert characterized it, is a shift "from a warfare to a lawfare context"—military coups are no longer necessary when courts can achieve the same results through constitutional rulings.

### Due Process in Political Cases

Due process protections erode significantly in politically sensitive cases through mechanisms that would be recognized as violations in ordinary criminal proceedings. Courts issue secret trial orders excluding the public from courtrooms despite international law guarantees of public proceedings. The justification offered—national security concerns in lese-majeste cases—creates categorical exceptions that swallow the rule.

> "Last year alone we've seen quite a number of secret trial orders being issued to kick members of the public out of the courtroom."
> — Thai Lawyers for Human Rights (INT06)

Defense subpoenas for evidence are systematically denied in Section 112 cases. When defendants seek to prove the truthfulness of their statements—a natural defense to defamation charges—courts refuse on grounds that the subject matter cannot be questioned. This creates a logical impossibility: defendants accused of false statements about the monarchy cannot present evidence that their statements were true because presenting such evidence would itself constitute further offense.

> "The persistent refusal of judges to issue subpoenas for the defense counsel... judges would not issue subpoenas in any of these cases. They would say, well, it's about the king, it's national security."
> — Thai Lawyers for Human Rights (INT06)

Bail is frequently denied pending trial or appeal in political cases, resulting in extended pre-trial detention that punishes defendants before any conviction and violates the presumption of innocence. Defendants have spent years in custody awaiting trial or appeal decisions. Political prisoners face severe conditions including limited access to healthcare and medication, restricted hygiene facilities, and inconsistent visitation rules across different detention facilities.

### Access Barriers and Relationship Culture

Even in non-political matters, access to justice is shaped by practical barriers that systematically disadvantage those without resources or connections. Civil litigation requires private legal representation—unlike the Labor Court, regular civil courts do not provide free lawyers—creating financial barriers that exclude many potential litigants from pursuing legitimate claims. Trials extend eight to ten months minimum, and winning produces only "a piece of paper" when enforcement against resistant defendants proves impossible.

> "If you're someone of influence or if you know someone or if you have connections with certain agencies, your matter or your case will go a lot quicker than the average Joe."
> — Commercial Lawyer (INT08)

Thai relationship culture—the social importance of personal connections and mutual obligation networks—affects treatment throughout the justice system and broader government dealings. Connected individuals receive expedited processing while others face standard delays. Processes that nominally take sixty days can be completed in twenty-four to forty-eight hours for those who know the right people. This creates differential access to justice based on social capital rather than legal merit.

> "People feel like they're hopeless about the justice system in our country because they don't know if it works for some group of people or for other citizens."
> — Civic Tech Expert (INT09)

What one expert called the "thin line with corruption" pervades interactions with official systems: the differential treatment based on relationships rather than formal rules affects both courts and government agencies. The practical consequence is that ordinary citizens who lack connections experience delays, obstacles, and inconsistent outcomes that undermine confidence in formal institutions and reinforce reliance on informal networks for getting things done.

## Conclusion

The expert perspectives gathered for this chapter reveal a Thailand in transition—but not necessarily in a consistent direction. Formal frameworks have strengthened while structural constraints persist. Progress in some domains (marriage equality, expanded assembly space, Labor Court accessibility, data protection) coexists with regression in others (political prosecution, civil society pressure, Constitutional Court intervention, enforcement failures). The country is simultaneously moving forward and backward depending on which dimension and which population one examines.

The central finding is that human rights in Thailand operate conditionally. For the majority of citizens engaging in non-sensitive activities and routine transactions with the state, the environment has improved and continues to function reasonably well. The formal infrastructure of rights protection—constitutional provisions, statutory frameworks, specialized courts, ombudsman offices—exists and operates with moderate effectiveness for ordinary matters. This explains why quantitative surveys may capture moderately positive public assessments of rights protection.

For those who engage with politically sensitive issues—or who belong to vulnerable groups including migrants, political activists, ethnic minorities in conflict regions, and human rights defenders—a different Thailand emerges. This Thailand features systematic surveillance, prosecution under expansive interpretations of repressive laws, courts that apply different standards for political cases, and institutional mechanisms designed to limit democratic accountability. The gap between public perception and expert assessment reflects not conflicting evidence but different exposures to a system that treats different populations under fundamentally different rules.

Understanding these dual realities—and the mechanisms that sustain them—is essential for interpreting both quantitative indicators and policy prospects. Thailand's challenge is not the absence of rights frameworks but the selective enforcement of existing protections in ways that insulate powerful institutions from accountability while leaving vulnerable populations exposed. The question for the future is whether the civic engagement awakened since 2020 can find channels for meaningful influence within institutional structures designed to limit precisely such influence, or whether the current equilibrium of conditional rights will persist.
