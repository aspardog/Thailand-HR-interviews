## Executive Summary

This joint interview with two UNDP representatives presents Thailand as "a story of two halves" - simultaneous progress and regression. The experts highlight landmark achievements (Marriage Equality Act, Anti-Torture Act, Business and Human Rights NAP) while identifying persistent challenges around freedom of expression, SLAPP suits, and labor rights. A key theme is the role of external drivers (EU FTA, OECD accession, HRC membership) in pushing reform. The interview provides valuable insight into the gap between legislative progress and lived experience, and the shift from "warfare to lawfare" in political control.

## Key Challenges [CHAL]

### 1. Strategic Lawsuits Against Public Participation (SLAPP)
**Dimension Code:** 1.3, 2.2, 4.1
**Sub-indicator:** Freedom of Expression; Non-Governmental Checks; Access to Civil Justice

**Description:** Nearly 100 business-related SLAPP cases documented over 20 years, with researchers estimating government-initiated cases could reach 1,000. The same companies repeatedly sue critics, particularly in mining and agriculture sectors.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "...just only the business related slab action we found that nearly you know 100 cases so far and when we dig deeper to the case, we found that you know just the same face of the company keep doing, you know, suing people."

> "They say that if you would like to also collect the data of the slab practice of the government authority. You know, can you imagine it's could up to 1000 case..."

**Vulnerable Groups:** VG-HR, VG-JN, VG-PA
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### 2. Section 112 (Lese-Majeste) as Absolute Restriction
**Dimension Code:** 1.3
**Sub-indicator:** Freedom of Expression and Opinion

**Description:** Article 112 creates an untouchable topic where even discussing reform leads to party dissolution and political bans, as demonstrated by the Move Forward Party case.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "...one of the issue that Thai people never ever, you know, been to speak up, you know, privately or no publicly is about the issue of the the majestic, the monarchy... one party has been dissolved, even they grained the majority vote because they they were, you know, criticized and were sued at the end by the Constitutional Court about, you know, promoting, you know, the reform of Article 112."

> "...when people hearing the word like you, you want to remove the Article 112, you know it's a big deal for for Thai, especially the the conservative people..."

**Vulnerable Groups:** VG-PA, VG-YT
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### 3. Computer Crime Act Used to Silence Critics
**Dimension Code:** 1.3, 1.6
**Sub-indicator:** Freedom of Expression; Privacy

**Description:** The Computer Crime Act is increasingly used for prosecution based on social media posts and reposts, combined with criminal defamation charges, creating a dual civil-criminal prosecution mechanism.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "...you have this Computer Crime Act which is increasingly being used and you can be then held accountable for something you say, something you're repost and face criminal, you know, charges and jail time without there being a very clear reason other than you've spoken up against the establishment."

> "...the state also kind of like using the existing, you know, legislation... you can post anything or even you just reshare the post. They can capture that as a evident and sue you in case of defamation..."

**Vulnerable Groups:** VG-JN, VG-HR, VG-PA, VG-YT
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

---

### 4. ILO Conventions 87 and 98 Not Ratified
**Dimension Code:** 3.1
**Sub-indicator:** Labor Rights

**Description:** Thailand has not ratified core ILO conventions on freedom of association and collective bargaining, meaning workers (especially migrants) cannot legally form unions.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "...still two main core ILO conventions that Thailand, you know in the process of ratifying... which is about, you know, freedom of association and also rise to collective bargaining Convention 87 and 98... under the Labor Protection law, we still have no rights for the worker to form the labor union."

> "...even the migrant workers, they have no right to forms any, you know, unions at the moment."

> "...most of the case when the, you know, the workers from the union, you know, start to negotiating or protest, they end up with the retaliations by the company."

**Vulnerable Groups:** VG-MW
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### 5. Shift from Warfare to Lawfare
**Dimension Code:** 4.2, 4.4
**Sub-indicator:** Improper Government Influence in Justice

**Description:** Political control has shifted from military coups to judicial mechanisms, with courts being used to achieve political outcomes.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "...it kind of shift from a a warfare to a lawfare context. And that's something that kind of a narrative that you hear here that it's now about lawfare. Um, that you know, there may not be a reason or need for a coup because you know, you can play out whatever needs to be played out through the court system as needed."

> "There was a lot of upset after the 2023 election, um, when the party that won was not in a position or not permitted to form a government and members of that party were banned from, uh, for politics for a decade."

**Vulnerable Groups:** VG-PA
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### 6. Informal Labor and Platform Workers Unprotected
**Dimension Code:** 3.1
**Sub-indicator:** Labor Rights

**Description:** Over 50% of workers are in the informal sector without social protections. Platform/gig workers (delivery, ride services) have no concrete legal protections.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "...the informal sector. The numbers of these worker in this sector now is account over 50% of the total worker which is like surprisingly... when this come to the informal sector, you know right that after the worker wouldn't be able to receive any social protections or benefit."

> "...these people, they call themselves as a freelance worker. You know, we still have no concrete laws to protect them from the violations and they haven't received a benefit..."

**Vulnerable Groups:** VG-MW
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### 7. Deep South Continues Under Security Restrictions
**Dimension Code:** 1.4, 1.5
**Sub-indicator:** Freedom of Assembly; Right to Life

**Description:** Southern border provinces operate under different rules with continuing security measures. Anecdote about helmet prohibition illustrates differential treatment.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "...for security reasons they're not allowed to wear helmets in the southern border provinces when they're riding on the motorbikes. And that really struck me, right? So it's one rule for everybody else... that little anecdote says an awful lot about it's a different context and a different sense of freedom and opportunity, particularly for Muslim communities..."

> "...there are special security measures that continue to be in place. There's the Martial Law Act, emergency decree on public administration... there is a curtailment of civil liberties as a result of that."

**Vulnerable Groups:** VG-MM
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-DS

## Key Highlights [HIGH]

### 1. Marriage Equality Act - Landmark Achievement
**Dimension Code:** 0.3, 1.2
**Sub-indicator:** Areas of Progress; Equality and Discrimination

**Description:** Thailand became the first country in Southeast Asia (second in Asia after Nepal) to pass marriage equality legislation, also raising marriage age to 18.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "...on 23rd January Twenty-five, Thailand passed the Equal Marriage Law, and this was a huge step forward... making Thailand the first country in Southeast Asia, I think the second in Asia after Nepal."

> "...it's beyond the question of marriage. It actually brings opportunities and equality in terms of inheritance rights, in terms of having children, in terms of access to health care and benefits..."

**Vulnerable Groups:** VG-LG
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### 2. External Drivers Pushing Reform
**Dimension Code:** 0.3, 0.4
**Sub-indicator:** Areas of Progress; Contextual Factors

**Description:** EU FTA negotiations, OECD accession, and HRC membership create pragmatic incentives for governance and human rights reforms.

> "...that external opportunity of OECD accession has brought the government, I think quite pragmatically to the the door of talking about some justice and governance related reforms..."

### 3. Business and Human Rights - NAP and HRDD
**Dimension Code:** 0.3, 3.1
**Sub-indicator:** Areas of Progress; Labor Rights

**Description:** Thailand was first country with stand-alone NAP on Business and Human Rights (2019), now moving toward mandatory human rights due diligence.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "Thailand has been, you know, the first country with the adoption of the stand-alone National Action Plan on Business and Human Rights since 2019..."

> "...the government would like to move forward from the mandatory, you know, NAP, a voluntary NAP to the mandatory, you know, registration. So now the government is in the process of drafting the mandatory human right due diligence."

**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA
