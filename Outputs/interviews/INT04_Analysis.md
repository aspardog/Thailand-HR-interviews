## Executive Summary

This academic expert provides a deeply critical assessment of Thailand's institutional and constitutional framework. The expert characterizes state institutions created to protect rights as "smokescreen" that fail to deliver on their promises. Key themes include the chilling effect of Article 112 (lese-majeste) prosecutions extending to MPs who merely proposed reforms, the rise of SLAPP suits against activists and academics, a "grand fatigue" among younger generations who see change as impossible, and regulatory capture embedded by design in institutions like the Competition Commission. The expert highlights a fundamental disconnect between surface-level modernization (digitalization, e-filing) and actual accountability.

## Special Note: Academic Perspective on Institutional Design

This expert provides a structural critique rarely articulated elsewhere:
- State institutions designed to appear protective but function as "smokescreen"
- Regulatory capture is **by design** (industry reps on regulatory boards)
- Cultural factors (hierarchical deference) structurally undermine independence
- Digitalization creates **unwilling** transparency without accountability
- The problems are "too intertwined to be resolved with a few laws"

This perspective suggests that surface-level reforms may be insufficient without addressing deeper institutional and cultural dynamics.
