## Executive Summary

This joint interview with two human rights NGO representatives provides critical ground-level perspective on labor rights and civic space. The experts emphasize that apparent freedoms are deceptive: "You don't know whether these freedoms are real or not" because prosecutions can come years after activities. Key themes include structural barriers to migrant worker organizing (can't form unions, undocumented workers have no remedies, millions affected), the weaponization of criminal law for private disputes and political control, and internal judicial hierarchy that undermines independence. Despite some legislative progress (sexual harassment law, anti-forced disappearance law), implementation gaps and selective enforcement persist. The overall assessment: "Open but limited."

### 1. Delayed Enforcement Creates False Sense of Freedom
**Dimension Code:** 1.3, 2.3
**Sub-indicator:** Freedom of Expression; Civic Participation

**Description:** Legal actions can be brought years after original speech or assembly, creating uncertainty that discourages sustained participation. People may feel free until prosecuted later.

**Supporting Evidence:**

> "...the space that you thought you had back then five years ago. It's not real. So probably... we didn't have it from the beginning. It's just some time we thought we had it, but once we realized it. The legal actions... coming non-stop."

### 2. Migrant Workers Cannot Form Their Own Unions
**Dimension Code:** 3.1
**Sub-indicator:** Labor Rights

**Description:** Migrant workers are legally prohibited from forming their own unions - they can only join existing Thai unions. Government treats migrants as security threat and refuses to ratify ILO conventions.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "Migrant workers can't form their own unions. So... that's one of the big problem... at most they could just join some other union."

> "...actually as Kay mentioned to be a migrant worker is... very impossible, sorry to say that, to have the union because Thailand... also see the migrant worker as a threat to national security... that's why they never ratified the ILO convention."

> "...if they give the power, it's mean that the migrant worker will like empower, you know. So that's why this is a way of like suppress this group of people."

**Vulnerable Groups:** VG-MW
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### 13. Over-Reliance on Criminal Law
**Dimension Code:** 4.6, 4.7
**Sub-indicator:** Due Process; Impartiality

**Description:** Thai system defaults to criminal rather than civil remedies, including for defamation. People use criminal law because it's free and maximizes harm to defendants, using taxpayer resources for private disputes.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "We still see the very classic problem about the over alliance on criminal law in the Thai justice system... when there's a defamation case, you could pick to go to criminal or civil... people would just always go to criminal law because they thought that, OK, I want to do it bad, I want to make it worse."

> "...it's free as well because you just have to go to the police and police do the rest of the things for you. So that mean we are using taxpayer money on defamation case like private defamation."

**Vulnerable Groups:** VG-HR
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### 15. Senior Judges Review and Pressure Junior Judges
**Dimension Code:** 4.2, 4.4
**Sub-indicator:** Improper Government Influence; Impartiality

**Description:** Senior judges review draft judgments and make recommendations. Junior judges feel pressure to conform. Cultural seniority norms reinforce hierarchy.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "...under Thai law they allow the senior judge to review the draft judgment of the junior judges. So that means the senior judge could decide the direction."

> "...they feel like they're under pressures to change the draft judgment to the way that senior recommended, not always the case... especially in 112 cases, several cases like that."

> "...in the Thai culture, if you agree or not agree, if you want feel OK, I disagree, but sometimes you have to agree because of the seniority."

> "...there was one time in which constitution prohibited judges to do this, to review draft judgment... just four or five years. And then that's military coup. And then once they draft a new constitution, they remove this because... senior judges, you know, they still want to have that power."

**Vulnerable Groups:** -
