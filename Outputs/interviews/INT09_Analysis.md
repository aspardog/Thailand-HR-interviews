## Executive Summary

This civic tech expert provides unique perspective on citizen participation and digital transformation. The overall assessment: **"Fundamentally human rights situation is okay, but there is still a lot that doesn't work the way it should."** Key themes include: progress since military government but conditional freedoms (ok unless touching monarchy/military/powerful interests), public consultations that are "ritual" without substantive influence, information access that is technically available but practically unusable (90,000 pages of scanned PDFs to answer one budget question), and digital divide affecting rural populations. The expert documents specific mechanisms: officials control information disclosure with year-long appeals, wealthy individuals receive delayed justice, and Constitutional Court decisions undermine democratic mandates. Civil justice is seen as better than criminal.

## Key Challenges [CHAL]

### 1. Sensitive Topics Create Zone of Silence
**Dimension Code:** 1.3
**Sub-indicator:** Freedom of Expression

**Description:** Expression is free for non-sensitive topics but strictly limited for monarchy, military, and powerful business interests. Creates two-tier system where political speech carries risk.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "...if you don't deal with some sensitive issues or deal with the like people with a lot of influence, it's OK... but if you talk about the sensitivity... we have the kind of strong Les Majeste law. If you talk about the monarchy in any way, you can have a chance to go to prison."

> "...we have people going to jail because of the issue every single month."

> "...even for the media... a lot of media that linked to that scrutinization, they get sued and they need to go on the kind of justice process for a long time."

**Vulnerable Groups:** VG-PA, VG-JN
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### 2. Political Parties Dissolved for Proposing Reform
**Dimension Code:** 1.3, 2.3
**Sub-indicator:** Freedom of Expression; Civic Participation

**Description:** Constitutional Court dissolved political party and banned politicians for campaigning on Les Majeste amendment. Even Parliament cannot deliberate on the topic.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "...last year the Constitutional Court said that even political parties cannot do that in campaigning... One political party in Thailand got dissolved because of this issue and a lot of politician got banned because they campaigned about the amendment of this law..."

> "...when they go to the Parliament, which should be a space that they can deliberate about this law, some conservative party said that we cannot do that because the Constitutional Court already made the decision."

**Vulnerable Groups:** VG-PA
**Geographic Scope:** GEO-NA

### 4. Official Information Act Controlled by Officials
**Dimension Code:** 2.4
**Sub-indicator:** Right to Information

**Description:** Despite freedom of information law, officials decide on disclosure. Appeals take 1+ year through multiple agencies and courts. Power remains with authority, not citizens.

**Supporting Evidence:**

> "...if we request something from the parliament, they deny... we need to appeal to the office of the cabinet. And then they may reject and we need to appeal to the court again."

### 6. Public Consultations as Ritual
**Dimension Code:** 2.3
**Sub-indicator:** Civic Participation

**Description:** Public hearings happen "because it requires" by constitution, but only before decisions. Citizens provide feedback, then closed circle makes decision without involvement or explanation.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "...so many times it's just a ritual that mean even you join the public hearing or the public forum, even in physical way... they do that because it requires... but it happens mostly before they make the decision."

> "...the friction is we don't know if our opinion or our feedback influence any of that decision because it's just a process that happened before and then we can do nothing..."

> "...after that it's a close circle that the board... they say they will use this feedback to discuss in close circle together and then they will announce what the result. So I think we don't have the kind of involvement during the period and after."

### 7. 10,000 Signature Complaint Mechanism Removed
**Dimension Code:** 2.1
**Sub-indicator:** Access to Complaint Mechanisms

**Description:** Previous constitution allowed collective petitions with 10,000 signatures to Parliament for oversight. Current constitution removed this article.

**Supporting Evidence:**
> "...in the past constitution, there's an article that if people... see that any state agency doesn't work effectively or not responsible for the people, they can gather together with 10,000 signature to propose that to the Parliament... after the amendment of this current constitution, this article disappear."

> "So right now... we have only way that's going to the court and they take a lot of effort..."

## Special Note: Civic Tech Perspective on Digital Transformation

This expert provides critical evaluation of digital participation:
- **What works**: Digital ID (Thai ID), e-signatures for citizen bills, linking tax information
- **What fails**: Google Forms for public hearings (excludes many), online-only participatory budgeting, scanned PDFs instead of searchable data
- **Root problem**: "It's not that inclusive if you do only digital format" - need hybrid approaches
- **Knowledge gap**: Rural communities don't know their rights - when explained, they participate

The expert has personally sued the Election Commission, demonstrating both access to justice and the 1.5-year timeline required.
