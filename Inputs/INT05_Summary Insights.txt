Thailand
Interviewees: INT06 and INT07
Quotes
* Tik: "You don't know whether these freedoms are real or not".
* Kay: "The overall picture for fundamental rights is open but constrained."
Civil & Political Rights
* Civic and political space is mixed and uneven. Compared with the period of military rule, there is more room for engagement through formal political processes and greater opportunity for dialogue with institutions. Parliamentary channels function more normally, and certain legislative and policy discussions have resumed. At the same time, these openings remain fragile and conditional. Legal tools continue to restrict expression and participation, and enforcement may occur long after the underlying activity. Individuals may speak or mobilize at one point in time but face investigations or prosecutions years later. This delayed enforcement creates uncertainty and discourages sustained public participation.
* Broadly framed laws continue to shape these constraints. Provisions related to public order, computer crimes, and national security allow selective application and are frequently used against activists, critics, and protest participants. Civic space appears open in form but remains vulnerable in practice.
* Some groups face more acute barriers than others. Migrant workers, particularly those without documentation, have limited ability to exercise these rights safely. Communities in the Deep South operate under a persistent security law environment that restricts movement, association, and expression. Hill tribe and indigenous communities face documentation gaps and limited access to consultation processes, particularly in development or infrastructure projects. Public hearings are often inaccessible or poorly designed, which reduces effective participation.
Participation and access to information 
* Civic participation is inconsistent and constrained by structural barriers, not by formal prohibitions alone. Engagement may be possible in principle, but opportunities to influence decisions are limited.
* Consultation processes are often procedural rather than substantive. Communities affected by projects may not receive information in accessible formats or languages, and consultation design can limit meaningful input. These gaps are most visible in rural and minority populations.
* Civil society capacity affects the quality of participation. Resource and funding constraints limit organizations' ability to monitor policies, advocate for reforms, or sustain long-term engagement. As a result, formal participation channels exist but have limited practical impact.
Labor and property rights
* Legal protections for labor rights do not consistently translate into practice. Migrant workers and subcontracted workers face barriers to organizing. Migrants cannot form their own unions and may only join existing ones. Subcontracted workers often lack legal standing to unionize within the enterprises where they work. These limits weaken collective bargaining.
* Union organizing carries risks. Employers may pressure or dismiss workers once registration efforts become known. Fear of job loss discourages organizing and reduces representation.
* Undocumented workers face the greatest obstacles. Many cannot safely use formal complaint mechanisms or pursue legal remedies and instead rely on NGOs for support. Social security benefits provide limited assistance but do not address broader labor violations or ensure redress.
* Government policy continues to treat migrant labor partly as a security issue. This approach restricts organizing and limits alignment with international labor standards.
Fair & Impartial Justice
* Concerns persist about the functioning and independence of the justice system, particularly in criminal cases. Criminal law is used in situations where civil remedies would be more appropriate. Criminal defamation and similar provisions are used to deter criticism and restrict the work of human rights defenders. Procedural safeguards intended to dismiss abusive litigation are available but applied infrequently.
* Independence challenges extend beyond the courts. Police and prosecutorial institutions remain subject to appointment processes and structures that allow political influence. These dynamics affect investigations and charging decisions before cases reach court.
* Internal judicial hierarchy also affects autonomy. Senior judges review draft judgments and provide guidance or recommendations, creating pressure on junior judges to conform. While framed as quality control, this process limits individual discretion. Norms of deference to seniority reinforce these constraints.
* These structural features reduce confidence in the justice system. Public trust declines where legal outcomes appear influenced by institutional or political considerations.
