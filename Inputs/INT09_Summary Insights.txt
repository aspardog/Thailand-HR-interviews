Thailand
Interviewees: Thanisara Ruangdej, CEO and Co-founder at WeVis. She agreed to be acknowledged by name in the report.
Quote
* "Fundamentally human rights situation is okay, but there is still a lot that doesn't work the way it should".
Civil & Political Rights
* Following the return to civilian government, civic space has become more open than during the period of military rule, but these improvements remain conditional. Public discussion is more visible, protests occur more regularly, and individuals can organize and participate in many social and political activities with fewer immediate restrictions than in the past. However, expression and participation depend heavily on the issue area. Speech and organizing around routine social or economic matters generally proceed without interference. Activities that involve the monarchy, the military, or other powerful political and economic actors continue to carry higher legal and personal risks. In these areas, individuals anticipate investigation, complaints, or prolonged legal proceedings, which encourages self-censorship even in the absence of formal sanctions.
* Freedom of assembly has increased in practice but remains regulated. Demonstrations and public gatherings take place more frequently, including protests outside ministries and government offices. Organizers typically coordinate with authorities and comply with notification requirements. While this marks a shift from the military period, the assembly is still shaped by permissions and informal constraints rather than as a fully protected right.
* Participation in formal political processes remains active, though opportunities to influence policy are limited. Civil society groups engage in civic campaigns and election related participation, and digital tools have lowered some logistical barriers to mobilization. At the same time, meaningful influence over policy outcomes is less certain. Public consultations often take place as a procedural requirement rather than a substantive exchange. Draft laws or regulations may be posted online without clear explanations or accessible summaries, limiting the ability of ordinary citizens to provide informed feedback.
Participation and access to information  
* Thailand maintains a formal legal framework for access to official information. In practice, however, obtaining government data remains slow and administratively burdensome. Requests frequently involve multiple steps, appeals, and long waiting periods. As a result, the process is difficult for ordinary citizens to use as a routine accountability mechanism. Information that is technically public is not always usable. Budget and policy documents are often released in formats that are difficult to search or analyze, such as large volumes of scanned files. This reduces the practical value of disclosure and raises the time and expertise required to scrutinize government decisions.
* Digitalization has produced mixed effects. Online systems have simplified certain interactions with the state. At the same time, digital-only processes risk excluding older populations, rural communities, and individuals with limited digital skills or time. Without offline or assisted options, technology can reinforce existing participation gaps.
* Confidence in complaint and oversight mechanisms remains limited. Formal complaint channels exist but are uneven and not widely trusted. Individuals who seek redress often rely on public pressure through media or social platforms instead of institutional remedies. Litigation represents the primary formal avenue for challenging decisions, but it is costly and time-consuming. Repeated court appearances, legal fees, and lengthy proceedings deter many people from pursuing cases.
Fair & Impartial Justice
* Concerns about institutional independence continue to affect perceptions of the rule of law. Decisions by constitutional and judicial bodies are widely viewed as having significant political implications, contributing to skepticism about whether courts operate fully independently from political or economic influence.
* Access to justice is also shaped by practical barriers. Individuals without financial resources or organizational support struggle to sustain legal challenges over time.
