Thailand
Interviewees: INT08
Quote
* "Human rights situation looks good, but it's a lot worse than it looks"
o After the recent elections and the apparent return to civilian government, conditions appear stable. This masks the continued scale of politically motivated prosecutions and the ongoing use of restrictive laws against dissent. Since 2014, these cases have followed a consistent pattern, with a sharp increase after the 2020 protest movement. Although fewer new cases are being filed, courts are issuing judgments in earlier cases, increasing the risk of imprisonment.
Civil & Political Rights
* Survey data show modest improvements in perceptions of right to life and personal security, as well as privacy. At the same time, these gains are not evenly distributed. Politically active individuals and human rights defenders continue to face higher risks. Individuals who criticize the monarchy, the military, or the government remain the most exposed.
* Right to life concerns arise in two areas. The number of offenses punishable by the death penalty has increased. Safeguards against refoulement are also applied inconsistently. Although Thai law prohibits returning individuals to countries where they face a real risk of torture or enforced disappearance, removals continue through executive decisions with limited judicial review.
* Political prosecutions continue to shape the civic space. From 2014 to 2019, Thai Lawyers for Human Rights recorded approximately 30-40 political cases per year. Following the 2020 youth-led protest movement, cases increased sharply, reaching several hundred within a short period. Since 2020, nearly 2,000 people have been charged or prosecuted under "lese-majeste", emergency regulations, the Public Assembly Act, and the Computer Crime Act. Prosecutions continued after the 2023 election. Courts are issuing judgments in earlier cases, increasing the likelihood of custodial sentences.
* Personal security and privacy remain concerns for activists. Police monitor individuals at home, at work, and at schools and universities. Authorities conduct visits and questioning, including of individuals who are no longer publicly active. Online monitoring is routine. Social media posts are documented and used to initiate complaints. Private pro-monarchy groups track online activity, collect personal information, and threaten legal action. Civil society organizations and individual defenders have reportedly appeared on military monitoring lists.
* Freedom of expression is uneven. Routine social and economic issues rarely attract enforcement. Speech related to the monarchy, military reform, or constitutional change carries a higher risk of investigation and prosecution. Criminal complaints, including defamation, are used to deter criticism. Strategic lawsuits against public participation (SLAPP-type cases) add further pressure. Thai legal procedures have also been used to pursue critics residing in Thailand on behalf of foreign actors, creating risks of cross-border legal harassment.
Participation and access to information  
* GPP survey shows improved ability to organize, attend meetings, and participate in political life, but civic participation remains uneven and depends largely on the issue area. Organizations working on education, social services, or community issues generally operate with fewer constraints. Groups working on monarchy reform, military accountability, or refugee protection face closer scrutiny. Registration of associations and foundations can be delayed or denied. Proposed legislation would expand oversight powers, increase compliance requirements, and create additional grounds for suspension or dissolution. Restrictions related to foreign funding add further uncertainty for civil society organizations.
* Protest dynamics have changed since 2020-2021. Large demonstrations initially raised expectations that street mobilization could lead to reform. Subsequent prosecutions, arrests, and lengthy sentences reduced participation. During the COVID-19 period, emergency powers were used to disperse assemblies and prosecute participants. Crowd-control measures included tear gas, water cannons, and rubber bullets. Although emergency regulations have ended, perceived risks associated with protest remain high.
* Civic engagement has shifted toward formal channels. Electoral participation remains strong. Volunteers monitor elections and report results. Constitutional amendment initiatives have mobilized broad public support, including rapid collection of physical signatures.
* Access to information also affects participation. Monitoring of online activity and complaint-driven enforcement discourage discussion of sensitive political issues. Limits on transparency in politically sensitive court proceedings reduce public oversight and restrict access to information about case handling.
* Financial constraints further limit civil society capacity. Reductions in external funding have led to downsizing and closures among several organizations. Activists facing multiple charges increasingly leave the country to avoid prosecution, contributing to a rise in politically motivated departures.
Fair & Impartial Justice
* Survey data show modest improvements in perceptions of the criminal justice system. Experiences differ, however, between ordinary cases and politically sensitive cases. Routine criminal and civil matters generally proceed through standard procedures. Politically sensitive cases are handled differently. Indictment rates in "lese-majeste" cases are exceptionally high, with few cases dismissed at the prosecutorial stage. Enforcement has not been consistent and varies with the political context.
* Judicial independence is affected by internal review practices and courtroom procedures. Draft decisions are submitted for hierarchical review before issuance. Judges consult court executives during recesses on matters such as bail, adjournments, and evidentiary requests. These practices add an additional layer of oversight in sensitive cases. The 2017 constitutional framework shapes the appointment and supervision of key judicial and accountability institutions and continues to influence public confidence in their independence. Constitutional reform is widely viewed as necessary to strengthen independence and accountability.
* Due process protections are uneven. Courts restrict public access or hold closed hearings in politically sensitive cases. Gag orders limit disclosure of proceedings. Defense requests for subpoenas or evidence are frequently denied, reducing the ability to challenge allegations. Bail is often denied or delayed, resulting in extended pre-trial detention and weakening the presumption of innocence.
* Political prisoners remain a key concern. Reported detention conditions include limited access to medical care and medication, restricted hygiene time, and inconsistent visitation rules across facilities. Given the number of prosecutions, amnesty has become an active policy issue. Draft legislation to grant amnesty for individuals prosecuted for political activity is key, but has not advanced.
